Editorial

Hofkirchner Wolfgang

A shift in opinion is taking place, in information science and other disciplines, in which the concept of information plays a central role. The need for this arises from the transformation processes to which our society is subject, and which are being pushed forward by the breakneck pace of developments in information and communication technologies. These processes tend to proceed spontaneously, without our full awareness, and are not accompanied by an equally rapid growth in scientific insight, let alone foresight. There is no science of the information society.

This change of view appeared five years ago in the efforts of some researchers to establish an exchange of ideas, brought about by the realisation that their considerations were breaking out of the boundaries of their respective fields. These pioneers included Mike Conrad of the Wayne State University, Koichiro Matsuno of the Nagaoka University of Technology, and Pedro Marijuán of the University of Zaragoza, all of whom were working at the interface of biology and computer sciences. Their efforts resulted in the organisation of a first international conference on the Foundations of Information Science in 1994 in Madrid. The circle of scientists who felt drawn by the subject grew ever larger; semiotics, system theory and general evolution theory were represented (in the form of some of their scientific organisations) in the preparations for FIS 96, the Second Conference on the Foundations of Information Science. This was hosted by the Social Cybernetics Group at the Institute of Design and Technology Assessment of the Vienna University of Technology.

The search was on for a unified theory of information. For me it is only natural that it was in Vienna, where unified science began, and the concept of evolution is just as alive as ever, that the following aims were presented: incorporating the reformulation of information theory into the radical changes which affect the natural and social sciences, cross-sectional and formal sciences, and philosophy; and finding a background theory for an as yet unformulated general theory of information within an evolutionary system theory, which itself is still to be created. It has become clear, however, that such complicated questions cannot be resolved by a simple show of hands at a single conference. In particular, the rift between those disciplines which count as precise, and those which are seen as less precise, remains to be solved.

The articles in this issue of INFORMATIK FORUM are presented against this background. It concerns the ability to listen to others, and accept their claim that the subject is relevant to other disciplines as well. The articles were chosen from fields which together represent a cross-section of modern science: physics, biology, psychology, sociology, technology, philosophy. Only by using transdisciplinary vision can we master the challenges presented by today¹s scientific and social developments.

I would like to take this opportunity to thank the Forschungsgesellschaft für Informatik, which not only supported the organisation of FIS 96, but also made possible the articles presented here.

Micro Time Reversal

Rössler Otto E., Diebner Hans H., Kittel Achim

Introduction

Boltzmann, Einstein and Everett provided relative-state formulations. Einstein always considered Boltzmann as his master. Boltzmann's idea is very simple: Two copies of the universe which differ only in the direction of time are one and the same universe. Leibniz and Boscovich could already have said the same thing, although they apparently did not do so.

Boltzmann's finding has an important corollary. A time reversal in a subsystem of the universe is equivalent to a time reversal in the rest of the universe. In particular, a time reversal in the observer is equivalent to a time reversal in the external world.

At first sight, this is an innocuous statement since a "time reversal in the observer" seems as unlikely an event to expect as a time reversal in the whole universe. Note that the latter requires that all particles in the universe reverse their directions of motion simultaneously. This could only occur spontaneously if all particles collided simultaneously in such a way that all momentums become zero at the same moment - a virtually infinitely unlikely event. The same argument at first sight applies to an observing subsystem.

Only if the observer is very simple - a single oscillator -is it trivially correct that relative to it, the world undergoes a time reversal after every half period.

In the following, it will be shown that the "single-pendulum observer" actually is a prototype for a much larger class of observers - including possibly ourselves. Boltzmann's microscopic relativity would then force a radically new view of reality upon us.

Introns and Development

Claudio Zamitti Mammana

Abstract

In this communication, the Law of Transformational Morphogenesis is introduced. This law which has been used implicitly in computer simulation of natural forms, provides the required framework to reduce to Genetics the transformational geometry mechanisms identified by D'Arcy Thompson as a key concept to characterize growth processes. Combined with two companion hypotheses of considerable explicative power (that codes for form and substance are largely independent in the genes and that introns code for morphogenesis), the transformational morphogenesis approach allows the reduction of Morphology, Embryology and Morphological Phylogeny to Molecular Genetics where Homology, ageing, natural death and some other developmental phenomena and abnormalities arise as natural consequences of the mechanisms identified by the present theory.

An Experiment with Pragmatic Information

Walter von Lucadou

Abstract

The model of pragmatic information (MPI), which is a candidate for a non-classical model of psychology, predicts that the behavior of a non-classical system depends on the conditions of its observation. The exchanged pragmatic information (meaningful information) ties the "observer" (e.g. a person) and the "observed" (e.g. a machine) together and creates an "organizational closed system". It is assumed that this process produces non-local correlations between the observer and the observed.

As a target to demonstrate this "observer effect", a quantum physical random event generator (REG) was used which was observed by human subjects who had the instruction to "influence mentally" the outcome of the observed REG.

The "observers" are characterized by a set of psychological variables which were measured by questionnaires before the observation-experiment. The "observed" is characterized by a set of statistical variables describing the observed random process. An "observer effect" should show up in correlations between these two sets of variables.

The results support the assumptions of the MPI that a human observer (or other self-organizing systems?) may select certain noise fluctuations from a seemingly separated system via non-local correlations if a feedback-loop generates "meaning" of the process to the observer. The process can be adequately described by the concept of "pragmatic information". It is shown that the concept of "pragmatic information" is a much better descriptor of the observation process than any other "Shannon-type" measure of information. The correlations between psychological and physical variables reflect the pragmatic information of the display and the instruction given to the subjects.

Keywords

  • non-classical models of psychology
  • pragmatic information
  • non-local correlations
  • organizational closure
  • observer effect

There is Plenty of Information Available but - What For?

Elohim Jiménez-López

Abstract

In our times most of us seem to be certain that it is impossible to avoid the appearance of something else in the future. Such kind of belief leads to assume that things in the earthly space cannot remain exactly the same while our Planet continues turning around itself and around our Sun. Then it seems evident that new things will emerge unavoidably here and there while some other things that had existed previously have not other chance than to disappear inexorably. On the other hand some of us have been learning in addition that events in the future will raise consequently from previous circumstances, necessarily conditioned and altered by actions happening at present, which could have been conceived purposefully aiming to get something else in the future.

However it is convenient to notice that the future for human individuals and communities may arise from circumstances significantly moulded during their respective existences by means of actions conceived by only a couple of individuals performing intentionally active. Gradually actions of this kind have made possible the emergence of a man-made World superimposed on the natural one. This World is now essentially the human habitat built and rebuilt by means of an increasing number of technological 'facilities' provided by peculiar circumstances created by the Western civilization, the prevailing one since several centuries ago. Today it is quite evident that the future of many of us in particular and the future of our World is largely determined by “the technological imperative which, like natural selection and evolution, inevitably leads where it will and precludes purposeful change, directed progress.³ [1].

Then, according to the trends of this civilization, in very recent years it is increasingly claimed or simply alleged that humankind is at the dawn of the information age. An age created by technological facilities which will make available the information about everything to anybody willing to comprehend and solve all kinds of problems confronted by men everywhere. Such perspective has made necessary to find out ways to deal scientifically with this kind of intellectual resources. Consequently it has been considered viable to constitute what might be the Information Science.

So far so good, but I dare to wonder why most scientists, practitioners, experts or educators are not more extensively and deeply involved in learning faster how to use effectively the huge amount of information already available for trying to comprehend and solve gradually, consistently and congruously the growing number of critical and very complex problems that many more humans have been facing inevitably along this century.

When thinking over specific panoramas of this kind it seems proper to bear in mind Popper's assumption that “We are not students of some subject matter but students of problems. And problems may cut right across the borders of any subject matter or discipline.³ [2]. Then it might be upheld that the intended Information Science will emerge 'naturally' and necessarily only when the information available about the problems of millions and millions of men & women is employed 'cleverly' for conceiving, designing and implementing feasible solutions, and also for improving effectively them.

Up to the present almost nobody can deny the magnificent advances of most technologies produced by the 'high intelligence' that could emerge in our planet. Neither could it be ignored that information technology is one of the intelligent outcomes of human brains. However, 'technology' is intrinsically neither humanitarian nor humane, despite it is the outcome of human thinking, reasoning & action. Consequently, supported by many evidences, it is now advisable for many of us to engage in thinking and action for tackling the serious survival problems that the Homo Sapiens Sapiens might be facing in the near future, due to an undeniable fact: “Technology is 'sold' as progressive and where technology goes society follows³ [1], but without noticing in advance that it, the technology, is generating side effects that become an obstacle to the evolvement of humanitarian concerns.

Such panoramic view, though relatively hypothetical, obliges scientists, practitioners, experts, educators, artists,... to search out scientifically how to use cleverly the intellectual possibilities provided by the so called Information Technology in order to conduct consciously and humanly (i.e. ethically and ecologically) the evolvement of our technological civilization.

Business Process Redesign

Graham Orange, Peter Dixon

Abstract

In this paper we set out to describe a research project upon which we have recently embarked. The project involves six different organisations, five commercial enterprises and two academic institutions. Our research is centred a-round the construction industry and will be seeking to integrate cross organisational functions and build an environment of trust and co-operation between the collaborating companies through the implementation of new working relationships and practices supported by the introduction of information technology.

This paper examines some of the current research relating to the research project. We explore the areas of Electronic Data Interchange (EDI), Interorganisation Systems (IOS), information strategy, business process redesign and then examine the important relationships between each of these elements. We then look at how this research fits within the context of our own research project and within the construction industry.

There are five commercial organisations collaborating in the project. The principal organisation is Taylor Wood-row Construction Ltd. The other organisations include two of Taylor Woodrow¹s major customers; a large retail outlet and Thames Water, a large publicly owned utility; the remaining two organisations are consultants in the design and economics of construction projects. Each organisation has a major role to play in the design process of a construction project. The success of the project is dependent upon organisations such as these working in partnership.

The research will be primarily carried out by the two academic institutions; The London School of Economics and Leeds Metropolitan University, with additional re-searchers provided by Taylor Woodrow Construction Ltd.

Editorial (German)

Hofkirchner Wolfgang

Es gibt ein Umdenken, nicht nur in der Informatik, auch in anderen Disziplinen, in denen der Informationsbegriff eine zentrale Rolle spielt. Der Bedarf dafür ergibt sich aus den Transformationsprozessen, denen unsere Gesellschaften unterliegen und die durch die rasanten Entwicklungen auf dem Gebiet der IuK-Technologien angestoßen werden ­ Prozesse, die aber eher spontan und unbewußt verlaufen und nicht von einer ebensolchen raschen Entwicklung wissenschaftlicher Einsicht, ganz zu schweigen von Voraussicht, begleitet werden: Es gibt keine Wissenschaft von der Informationsgesellschaft.

Bereits vor fünf Jahren begann ein Umdenkprozeß bei Forscher und Forscherinnen, die merkten daß ihre Überlegungen die Grenzen ihrer Fächer zu überschreiten beginnen. Zu ihnen gehören Mike Conrad von der Wayne State University, Koichiro Matsuno von der Technischen Universität Nagaoka und Pedro Marijuán von der Universität von Zaragoza, alle an der Schnittstelle Computerwissenschaften/Biologie arbeitend. Sie luden zur ersten internationalen Konferenz über die Grundlagen der Informationswissenschaft 1994 in Madrid ein. Die Semiotik, die Systemtheorie, die allgemeine Evolutionstheorie wurden in Gestalt einiger ihrer wissenschaftlichen Organisationen in die Vorbereitung der FIS 96, der Second Conference on the Foundations of Information Science, einbezogen, als deren Gastgeberin die Abteilung für Sozialkybernetik, der TU Wien fungierte.

Gefragt wurde nach einer vereinheitlichten Theorie der Information. Es ist für mich nur natürlich, wenn am Tagungsort Wien, wo einstmals die Einheitswissenschaft verkündet wurde und der Evolutionsgedanke nach wie vor lebendig ist, das Angebot unterbreitet wurde, die Bestrebungen zur Reformulierung der Informationstheorie in die allgemeinen Umbrüche einzubetten, von denen die Natur- wie die Gesellschaftswissenschaften, die Querschnitts- und Formalwissenschaften und die Philosophie erfaßt werden, und die entsprechende Hintergrundtheorie einer erst zu schaffenden allgemeinen Theorie der Information in einer evolutionären Systemtheorie zu suchen, die, um die Begriffe Komplexität und Selbstorganisation herum, gleichfalls noch auszuarbeiten ist.

In diesem Sinne möchte ich die mit dieser Ausgabe des INFORMATIK FORUM vorgelegten Artikel, die in Zusammenhang mit der FIS 96 entstanden sind, verstehen. Es geht ums Zuhörenkönnen, um den Versuch, das zu verstehen, von dem der andere meint, daß es nicht nur in seiner eigenen Disziplin von Bedeutung ist. Die hier vorgestellten Beiträge kommen aus Fächern, die zusammen einem Querschnitt durch den heutigen Wissenschaftsbetrieb gleichkommen: Physik, Biologie, Psychologie, Soziologie, Technik, Philosophie. Nur wenn wir uns einen trans-disziplinären Blick aneignen, werden wir befähigt, mit den Herausforderungen, vor die uns die Wissenschafts- wie die Gesellschaftsentwicklung stellen, umzugehen.
