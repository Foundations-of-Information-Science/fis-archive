Antonio Drago and Emanuele Drago

  • Information Science as a Paradigmatic Instance of a Problem-Based Theory

    Department of Physical Sciences - University Federico II, Naples; v. F. M. Briganti 412, 80141, Naples

    It is apparently organized in a different way than the Aristotelian ideal, i.e., by means of an infinite sequence of deductive inferences from few selfevident principles. In the past, several authoritative scholars on the foundations of science - in particular, E. W. Beth - emphasized the misleading preconception of this unique ideal of organization of scientific theory.
    From a historical analysis on mathematical theories and physical theories evidence is collected for a new ideal of organization of a scientific theory. It was promoted by D'Alembert and then substantiated by the theories of classical chemistry, Lazare Carnot's geometry, calculus and mechanics, Sadi Carnot's thermodynamics, Lobachevsky's non-Euclidean geometry, Galois' theory-by bounding our list to the theories till up the first half of the 19th Century.
    By a comparison of such theories one obtains the following common features. The core of the theory is constituted by an universal problem (in S. Carnot's thermodynamics, the maximum efficiency of the heat conversion in work) whose solution is not at hand of the current science. In order to solve this problem a new scientific method is looked for. The starting point of this method is constituted by a double negated statement (in the previous theory, "It is impossible the motion without an end"), whose corresponding positive statement lacks of scientific evidence ("each motion will end" can be stated neither by a finite set of experiments nor by calculations). Hence, the law Ø Ø A=A fails, as it is the case in the intuitionistic logic or, more in general, in a non-classical logic. Such a double negated statement surely does not play the role of an axiom-principle because no anlytical consequences may be drawn from it. As a consequence, the full deductive scheme does not apply in just the starting step. Rather, it constitutes a methodological principle, from which an ad absurdum theorem follows (in S.Carnot's thermodynamics: Carnot's theorem), which reaches an universal conclusion (the highest efficiency of whatsoever process of heat conversion) which now constitutes the hard fact upon which one may deductively develop the subsequent theory.
    A program for discovering new foundations for the IT is illustrated according to the ideal of a problem-based theory. In particular, it is suggested the main problem under the form of a double negated statement, the character of an ab absurdo theorem is stressed for Shanon Theorem; and the role played by the symmetries in founding IT is suggested.

    Keywords: information theory, history of science, philosophy of science

Petros A. M. Gelepithis

  • A Rudimentary Theory of Information: Consequences for Information Science and Information Systems

    Kingston University, Kingston upon Thames, KT1 2EE, England.
    E-mail: petros@kingston.ac.uk

    The key fundamental notions of Information Science and Information Systems are 'information' and 'information system' respectively, with information the central common notion. Nevertheless, despite the huge literature on both 'information' and 'information system' their nature remains debatable and their conceptual nexus to each other and related notions like 'sign' and 'meaning' are, at best, fuzzy or incomplete.
    The aim of this paper is no briefly review the notions of 'information' and 'information system' and to outline a theory of information. Specifically, in section one, we provide a brief analysis of 'information system' and a summary presentation of the major views on the nature of 'information', concluding that: (i) 'information' and 'communication' constitute the backbone of any theory of information; and (ii) all the relevant studies of information are fragmented, failing to provide a unifying theory and, in particular, to clarify the highly debatable nature of information. In section two, we provide the elements of a rudimentary theory of information and draw some of the consequences of this preliminary body of knowledge for Information Science and Information Systems.

    Keywords: information, information system, meaning, communication, understanding, theory, artifical intelligence, human-machine systems, computer

Bela Antal Banathy

  • A Unifying Typology of Information

    International Systems Institute, and Saybrook Institute, 38 Seca Place, Salinas, CA 93908, USA

    It is argued that the traditional definitions of information are not suitable in the axiomatic base of information science. A new framework is proposed that subsumes the traditional definitions as a special case.
    The information theory of Shannon (and its derivatives) nicely cover the formal, state-determined domains for which they were crafted. Subsequent developments, primarily in evolutionary biology, cybernetics and systems theory have shown that the nature of living systems is such that traditional informational views cannot account for the salient phenomena; and that these accounts cannot be (re)constructed at higher (meta) levels of abstraction.
    Following the work of Csanyi and Kampis, a distinction is drawn between referential and nonreferential information. This typology is further extended to include statereferential information as a means of accounting for the entailment relationships between different types of informational processes. The statereferential type subsumes the traditional definitions, and positions the current information sciences and technologies within the overall framework.
    A synthesis of the above work results in information being defined as the organizing property of living (social) systems. This property is constituted of cohesive, selective, and active information types. Cohesive information (statereferential) provides cohesion across space and time, selective (nonreferential) is boundary enforcing, and active (referential) is process sustaining.
    The theoretical, methodological, and practical implications of the proposed definitions (and resulting conceptual framework) are briefly discussed.

    Keywords: information typology, referential, nonreferential, statereferential, evolution, organization, meaning, freedom from determinism

Josef Wallmannsberger

  • On Limits: Towards a Pototheory of Inform(ul)ation

    University of Innsbruck, A-6020 Innsbruck, Austria-Europe,
    E-mail: Josef.Wallmannsberger@uibk.ac.at

    The concepts of "limit" and "barrier" have moved to the focus of attention in a number of fields in the natural and social sciences. Division of intellectual labour - itself the product of a limit-creating move in the social manufacture of knowledge - has made it difficult to develop transdisciplinary approaches to the problems at hand. The paradigm of "information science" will provide opportunities for more global perspectives on the function of limits and barriers in fields of intellectual and social action. In this paper a first attempt will be made at presenting a theory of barriers for information science.
    The entropic economy of "nature" has an absolute limit because of the laws of thermodynamics. In the long run, a level of minimized general energy will be reached and all local disequilibria will have been erased. (But then, "in the long run" we are all dead.) This "ideal" world of thermodynamics operates against a background of the continued creation of energetic subsystems that seem to insulate themselves against the general trend. One of these subsystems is commonly called "life". Barriers in a plethora of forms play a decisive role in this process, ranging from membranes to autopoetic feedback loops. "Information" plays a decisive role in the development of local disequilibria.
    The role of barriers in social systems has been amply demonstrated and will thus be dealt with summarily in this paper. The problematics of "inside-outside" defines a crucial point in the developmen of any social formation. It will be argued that the "foreigner" (xenos) articulates a complex cognitive and social field. The often competing strategies informing the "inside-outside"-matrix of a particular community will be examined. It will be argued that the psychoanalytic concept of the "other" has considerable metatheorerical potential for a general theory of "information."
    The main focus of the paper will be on the importance of "barriers" in processes of the social manufacture of knowledge. It will be argued that a theory of "barriers" is motivated by a number of seemingly diverse areas, such as gestalt psychology and Goedel's theorems on undecidability. Information processing environments have to supply tools or mechanisms for creating "barriers". The need for a type of general "locality" indicator runs through a large number of intellectual projects. Finally, proposals will be made how "barriers" can be integrated into a more global theory of the situatedness of knowledge. In a self-reflexive turn, general information science will radically deconstruct the concept of "generality" in science.

    Keywords: Limits, boundaries, communication theory, information science and linguistics.

  • The Overall Pattern of the Evolution of Information in Dissipative, Material Systems

    Natural Systems, HCR 86, 228 Laurel Bank Avenue, Deposit, NY 13754

    I have argued for the reality of a second law of infodynamics. That is, that the information carrying in a system, as viewed from within, must continue to increase as long as the system supports internal observation. I will be concerned here with matters of scale, as well as with system senescence (rigidity produced by the asymptotic approach to maximum information storage within a system). My major categories derive from a view of system development, and are: immaturity, maturity and senescence. As an immature system grows in size, it deploys information over ever smaller scales as it transforms from the vagueness of immaturity toward ever more definite embodiment. This eventually results in an information overload, gradually eroding system adaptability as it increasingly inhibits the variety of system responses by limiting its ability to further qualify stored information.

    Keywords: development, disorder, entropy, externalist, immaturity, infodynamics, internalist, meaning, order, organization, scalar hierarchy, senescence, vagueness

  • Entropy, Information and Predictability of Evolutionary Systems

    Institut of Physics, Humboldt-University Berlin

    We investigate first several entropy concepts, from static to dynamic entropy. Dynamic entropy is introduced as a measure of predictability. Then the relation between entropy and information is studied. Following Wiener information is considered as a non-physical quantity, not matter or energy. Information is understood as a binary relation between a sender and a receiver. We differ between bound and free information. Information can be created by selforganization, historically it is connected with the origin of life. The structure and predictability of informational strings is investigated. For example we study symbolic sequences generated by evolution, as e.g. texts. It is shown that several information carriers show criticality connected with the existence of long-range correlations, long memory in time and historical behaviour (self-tuned criticality). The higher order Shannon entropies and the conditional entropies (dynamical entropies and there limit) are calculated, characteristic scaling laws are found.

    Keywords: life, bound information, free information, thermodynamic entropy, statistical entropy, Shannon entropy, energy, evolution

Gerhard Grössing

  • Quantum Information in an Evolutionary Perspective

    Austrian Institute for Nonlinear Studies, Parkgasse 9, A-1030 Vienna, Austria,
    E-mail: x0371daa@awiuni11.bitnet

    An approach to quantum phenomena is reviewed that deals with the possibility of their realistic interpretation in the sense that they represent manifestations of hermeneutic circles between quantum "objects" and their experimental boundary conditions. Quantum cybernetics provides an evolutionary perspective in that all higher-level organizations like molecules, cells, living systems, etc., can be discussed under one and the same systemic viewpoint: a hermeneutic circularity between a "core" (or "nucleus") and a relevant "periphery" (or "environment") which constitutes the systems' organization and information potential.
    Generally, in realistic theories, an individual quantum system is analyzable into a local "particle-like" nonlinearity of a generally nonlocal "wave-like" mode of some sub-quantum structure of the vacuum ("Dirac ether"). In this view, a "particle" can be considered as being "guided" along one specific route by the (generally nonlocal) configurations of superimposed waves, which spread along all possible paths of an experimental setup. Moreover, in the approach of Quantum Cybernetics, an additional focus is given on the fact that the energy and momentum of a particle also determine the wave behavior. Thus, "waves" and "particles" are mutually and self-consistently defined, and Quantum Cybernetics puts particular emphasis on the circular relationship - mediated by plane waves - between a quantum system and its macroscopically defined boundary conditions.

    Keywords: hermeneutic circles, quantum systems, cybernetics
